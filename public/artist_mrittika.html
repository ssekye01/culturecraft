Artist Profile

Mrittika Rahman

Clay Tales of Bengal • Sculpture and pottery

Background

Mrittika Rahman grew up in Old Dhaka, where narrow lanes echoed with the sounds of rickshaw bells, temple conch shells, and the rhythmic tapping of craftsmen shaping clay in roadside workshops. Her mother, a schoolteacher from Barisal, often brought home hand-molded pots from village markets, and these everyday objects sparked Mrittika’s fascination with texture, storytelling, and the warmth of terracotta. As a child, she would spend evenings watching local artisans sculpt idols for Puja festivals, absorbing their techniques and the narratives woven into each figure. After studying fine arts at the University of Dhaka, Mrittika combined her formal training with the folk styles of southwestern Bangladesh, where terracotta carving has deep historical roots. She is especially drawn to the expressive simplicity of rural life—the curve of a boat on the river, the flowing drape of a sari, or the vibrant expressions of Baul musicians. Today, her studio in Dhaka is filled with earth tones, wooden tools, and trays of red clay sourced from Kushtia and Bogura, which she shapes into contemporary renditions of age-old Bangladeshi motifs.



What inspires this collection

The Clay Tales of Bengal collection is Mrittika’s homage to the stories carried by the soil of Bengal—tales of songs, harvests, rivers, and community. Pieces like the Baul Singer Figurine and Boat Procession Panel celebrate the musical and riverine heritage of rural Bangladesh, capturing moments of joy, rhythm, and collective movement. Her Ganga Fisherwoman Lamp honors the labor and resilience of women who work by the riverside, while works such as the Harvest Day Wall Plate reflect her deep admiration for the agricultural traditions that sustain village life. Mrittika often incorporates alpana-style borders and floral motifs, visible in creations like the Alpana Motif Coasters and Lotus Ghat Incense Holder, blending domestic artistry with spiritual symbolism. She sees terracotta as a medium that carries memory—of mud under one’s feet during monsoon, of festival nights lit by clay lamps, and of boats gliding across quiet canals. Through this collection, she seeks to preserve these small but enduring moments, offering viewers a sense of home, heritage, and the timeless beauty of Bangladeshi craft.
