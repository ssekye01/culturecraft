Artist Profile

Ren Sakamoto

Treasures of Japan • Sculpture and artifacts

Background

Ren Sakamoto was born in Kyoto and raised in a family of traditional craftspeople, where the scent of hinoki wood, the sound of metal chisels, and the indigo vats of textile dyers were all part of everyday life. His father restored machiya townhouses, and Ren spent much of his childhood sketching their latticed windows and tiled rooftops. His grandmother, a calligrapher from Osaka, taught him to appreciate the balance between simplicity and intention—lessons that shaped his artistic vocabulary early on. As a teenager, Ren became captivated by the textures and rhythms of handmade objects: the cool weight of ceramic cups, the softness of hand-dyed cloth, the patina of carved wooden talismans. He later studied industrial arts in Tokyo, where he blended traditional Japanese aesthetics with contemporary functionality. Today, Ren works from a quiet studio in the northern foothills of Kyoto, surrounded by shelves of ceramics, bamboo brushes, wind chimes, textiles, and lacquered trays. His work reflects the understated refinement of Japanese craft, with a focus on form, balance, and natural materials.



What inspires this collection

Treasures of Japan is Ren’s ode to the everyday beauty found in Japanese life—from the peaceful rituals of morning tea to the red gates of ancient shrines. Pieces like the Azure Wave Sake Set and Sakura Evening Tea Bowl express his love of traditional pottery, drawing on influences from Hokusai’s wave motifs and the soft pink glow of Kyoto’s spring blossoms. Textile works such as the Shibori Indigo Furoshiki and Indigo Noren Doorway Curtain reflect his fascination with indigo dyeing, a craft he learned from artisans in Tokushima who taught him the patience behind each fold, knot, and dye bath. Many items in the collection carry a sense of movement and gentle nostalgia. The Kintsugi Moon Plate celebrates imperfection and renewal, while the Ukiyo-e Fan Wall Art and Tokyo Street Lantern draw from Edo-era woodblock prints and the glowing lantern signs of Tokyo alleyways. Sculptural objects like the Kokeshi Sisters Duo, Red Torii Desk Shrine, and Kyo Machiya Incense House speak to his lifelong connection to Japan’s architecture and folk traditions. Through this collection, Ren hopes to share pieces that feel both rooted in history and deeply personal—small objects that carry calm, craftsmanship, and a moment of Japan into everyday spaces.
